## Understanding Sod Measurements and Pallet Sizes

Before you grab your measuring tape, let's talk about how sod is sold. In Jacksonville and throughout Northeast Florida, sod is typically sold by the pallet, not by individual square feet. Understanding pallet sizes is essential for accurate ordering.

A standard sod pallet covers between 400 and 500 square feet, depending on the grass variety you choose. St. Augustine varieties like Floratam and Palmetto typically come on 400-500 square foot pallets, while Bermuda grass pallets (including popular varieties like TifTuf and Celebration) often contain closer to 500 square feet. Zoysia varieties like Empire and Zeon usually fall in the 450 square foot range per pallet.

Individual sod pieces are typically cut in rectangular strips that measure 16 inches wide by 24 inches long, though this can vary slightly by farm. These pieces are stacked and rolled onto wooden pallets for transport. At Jax Sod, we've standardized our pallets to ensure consistent coverage, but it's always worth confirming the exact square footage per pallet when you place your order.

> **Jacksonville Pro Tip:** When calculating for Bahia grass (common in budget-conscious projects or rural properties in Clay County), expect pallets to contain about 400 square feet. Bahia is typically sold in larger, heavier pieces than St. Augustine.

## How to Measure Your Jacksonville Yard

Accurate measurements are the foundation of ordering the right amount of sod. Most Jacksonville properties can be broken down into simple geometric shapes that make calculation straightforward.

### Measuring Rectangular Areas

Start with the easiest spaces: rectangles. Most front yards, backyards, and side yards are essentially rectangular, even if they don't look perfectly square. Use a measuring tape (a 100-foot tape makes this much easier) and measure the length and width of each rectangular section.

For a simple rectangular backyard that measures 50 feet by 30 feet, your calculation is straightforward: 50 x 30 = 1,500 square feet. Write down each measurement as you go, labeling what area of your property you're measuring (front yard, side yard, backyard, etc.).

If your yard has curves or isn't perfectly rectangular, approximate by measuring to the general boundary. It's better to slightly overestimate these irregular edges than to underestimate, since you'll end up with gaps.

### Breaking Down Irregular Shapes

Jacksonville neighborhoods like Riverside and Avondale often feature properties with interesting angles, curves, and non-standard lot shapes. The key is breaking these spaces into manageable geometric shapes.

For triangular areas (common around corner lots or properties with angled fencelines), measure the base and the height. The area of a triangle is base x height ÷ 2. So a triangular side yard with a 20-foot base and 15-foot height would be 20 x 15 ÷ 2 = 150 square feet.

For circular or curved areas (like around trees, patios, or curved driveways common in Ponte Vedra developments), measure the diameter of the circle and use the formula: radius squared x 3.14. If you have a circular area around a tree with a 10-foot diameter, the radius is 5 feet, so 5 x 5 x 3.14 = 78.5 square feet.

For truly irregular shapes, divide the area into multiple rectangles and triangles. Measure each section separately, calculate the square footage, and then add them all together.

## Subtracting Non-Sodded Areas

Once you've measured the total area, you need to subtract spaces that won't be sodded. This step is crucial to avoid over-ordering.

Common areas to subtract include:

- **Hardscaping:** Driveways, sidewalks, patios, pavers, pool decks
- **Landscape beds:** Mulched areas, flower beds, shrub plantings
- **Structures:** Sheds, playsets, air conditioning units, generators
- **Water features:** Ponds, fountains, drainage areas
- **Non-lawn areas:** Gravel areas, dog runs, vegetable gardens

For each of these spaces, measure the area using the same techniques (rectangles, triangles, circles) and subtract from your total. If your backyard is 2,000 square feet but has a 300-square-foot patio and 200 square feet of landscape beds, your actual sod need is 2,000 - 300 - 200 = 1,500 square feet.

Many Jacksonville homeowners forget to account for landscape beds and end up with excess sod. Take the time to measure everything carefully. At Jax Sod, we've found that typical Southside properties have about 15-20% of their total lot dedicated to non-lawn areas, while newer Nocatee homes might have 25-30% due to larger driveways and extensive landscaping.

## Converting Square Footage to Pallets

Now that you have your total square footage, it's time to convert to pallets. This is where knowing your grass variety matters.

Use these general conversions based on common pallet sizes in Northeast Florida:

- **400 sq ft per pallet:** Divide your total square footage by 400
- **450 sq ft per pallet:** Divide your total square footage by 450
- **500 sq ft per pallet:** Divide your total square footage by 500

For example, if you need 3,600 square feet of St. Augustine and each pallet contains 450 square feet: 3,600 ÷ 450 = 8 pallets exactly.

Always round up to the nearest whole pallet. If your calculation gives you 7.3 pallets, order 8. Sod farms can't split pallets, and it's better to have a little extra than to run short mid-installation.

> **Jacksonville Pro Tip:** For large projects over 10 pallets in neighborhoods like Deerwood or Town Center, we often recommend a site visit to confirm measurements. Jacksonville's sandy soil can create subtle grading challenges that affect actual coverage needs.

## Accounting for Waste and Overage

One of the biggest mistakes Jacksonville homeowners make is ordering exactly the calculated amount without any buffer. You need to account for waste, which occurs from several sources during installation.

**Standard waste factors:**
- **5% waste minimum:** For simple rectangular yards with straight edges
- **7-10% waste:** For properties with curves, angles, or multiple sections
- **10-15% waste:** For complex landscapes with many obstacles, irregular shapes, or slopes

Waste comes from cutting sod to fit around obstacles, trimming edges, and accounting for pieces that tear or get damaged during installation. In Jacksonville's summer heat, sod that's exposed too long can also dry out at the edges, creating additional waste.

To calculate waste, multiply your square footage by the waste percentage. For a 2,000-square-foot yard with 10% waste: 2,000 x 0.10 = 200 square feet of additional sod needed, for a total order of 2,200 square feet.

When converting to pallets, add the waste to your total before dividing. Using the example above with 450-square-foot pallets: 2,200 ÷ 450 = 4.89, which rounds up to 5 pallets.

## Ordering Tips for Jacksonville Projects

Once you've calculated your needs, keep these ordering tips in mind to ensure a smooth installation.

**Timing matters:** Order sod to arrive the same day you plan to install. Sod is a living plant and begins to deteriorate once cut. In Jacksonville's summer heat (often reaching 95°F with high humidity), sod sitting on a pallet can suffer heat stress in just a few hours. We recommend scheduling delivery for early morning (before 8 AM if possible) so you can install during the cooler part of the day.

**Confirm delivery access:** Make sure delivery trucks can access your property. Standard sod delivery trucks need at least 12 feet of clearance and turnaround space. For properties in historic neighborhoods like Riverside or Avondale with narrow streets or tight driveways, discuss delivery logistics with your supplier ahead of time. Having a quarter or half pallet extra is normal and can be used to repair bare spots, patch edges, or fill in areas that settle.

**Consider installation help:** While DIY installation is possible for small projects, anything over 5 pallets becomes physically demanding work. ## Common Measuring Mistakes to Avoid

After three decades serving Jacksonville homeowners, we've seen these measurement mistakes repeatedly:

**Forgetting slope and elevation changes:** Sloped areas require more sod than flat areas because you're covering angled ground. For moderate slopes common in Mandarin and St. Johns County properties, add an extra 5-10% beyond standard waste factors.

**Ignoring settlement:** Newly graded soil settles, especially Jacksonville's sandy soil. Areas that look level during measuring may sink slightly, creating low spots that need extra sod. If you've recently had fill dirt delivered or grading work done, wait a week and water the area to let it settle before final measuring.

**Measuring to the old grass line:** If you're replacing dead or damaged grass, the previous lawn edge might not be where you want your new lawn to end. Consider whether you want to expand into mulch beds, extend closer to sidewalks, or adjust the lawn boundary before measuring.

**Miscalculating curves:** Homeowners often underestimate curved areas, especially around large trees or curved bed edges common in Ponte Vedra and Jacksonville Beach properties. When in doubt, treat curves as rectangles and add extra waste percentage.

**Not accounting for sod thickness:** While this doesn't affect square footage calculations, remember that new sod adds about 1-2 inches of height to your yard. This can affect mowing strip height, sprinkler head levels, and edging against sidewalks and driveways.
